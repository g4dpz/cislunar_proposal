Extending the RADIANT Vision: A Proposal for Future Development

Introduction

The RADIANT (Radio Amateur Delay-tolerant Interplanetary Networking Testbed) project was established to explore the practical application of Delay-Tolerant Networking (DTN) technologies within the amateur radio community. The current project phases remain focused on validating core DTN concepts, integrating emerging standards such as Bundle Protocol Version 7 (BPv7), and demonstrating how amateur radio infrastructure can participate in future space networking architectures.

As the project progresses through its planned development stages, it is appropriate to begin considering how the architecture might evolve beyond the initial implementation objectives.

This paper does not propose a change in direction for RADIANT. Rather, it outlines a possible future extension that builds upon the project’s existing goals and achievements.


The Current RADIANT Vision

The current RADIANT architecture is focused on demonstrating and evaluating DTN technologies in an amateur radio environment.

At a high level, the architecture can be represented as:

Applications
      │
      ▼
DTN Node
      │
      ▼
Amateur Radio Network
The current objectives include:

Evaluating BPv7 operation over amateur radio links.
Investigating store-and-forward networking techniques.
Exploring scheduled-contact communications.
Providing an educational platform for DTN experimentation.
Developing experience relevant to future cislunar and deep-space communications.
These objectives remain the primary focus of the project.


Looking Beyond Individual DTN Implementations

As RADIANT matures, an important architectural question emerges:

Should RADIANT be tied to a specific DTN implementation, or should it evolve to support multiple implementations?

Today, several DTN implementations exist, including:

HDTN
ION-DTN
ESA DTN
Each offers different strengths and operational characteristics.

While selecting an initial implementation is necessary for practical development, future interoperability may become increasingly important as DTN adoption expands.

This suggests that RADIANT could eventually benefit from an abstraction layer that separates RADIANT services from the underlying DTN engine.

Conceptually:

Applications
      │
      ▼
RADIANT Services
      │
 ┌────┼────┐
 │    │    │
 ▼    ▼    ▼
HDTN ION ESA DTN
Such an approach would allow experimentation with multiple DTN implementations while preserving a consistent application environment.

At present this should be viewed as a future architectural consideration rather than an immediate development objective.


Contact Plans as a Shared Resource

One of the recurring themes in DTN operations is the importance of contact information.

DTN networks differ from conventional IP networks because connectivity opportunities are often predictable and scheduled.

Examples include:

Satellite passes.
Ground station visibility windows.
Scheduled relay opportunities.
Future cislunar communications links.
Today, contact plans are typically managed within individual DTN implementations.

A possible future extension would be to treat contact information as a shared network resource managed independently of the DTN implementation itself.

Under such a model:

Mission Planning
        │
        ▼
RADIANT Contact Service
        │
 ┌──────┼──────┐
 │      │      │
 ▼      ▼      ▼
HDTN   ION   ESA DTN
The contact service would become the authoritative source for network opportunities while individual DTN implementations remain responsible for forwarding bundles.

This concept might be described as:

Contact Plan as a Service (CPaaS)

Although not currently part of the project roadmap, it represents an interesting area for future investigation.


Lessons from Ground Station as a Service

The amateur satellite community has already experienced a similar transition.

Historically, operators interacted directly with individual ground stations.

Projects such as the GENSO initiative and later commercial Ground Station as a Service (GSaaS) platforms demonstrated a different approach.

Instead of requesting access to a specific antenna, users request a communication opportunity.

The infrastructure then determines how that opportunity is fulfilled.

A comparable approach could eventually be applied to DTN networks.

Rather than focusing on individual links or nodes, applications could express requirements such as:

destination: LunarNode1
priority: High
delivery_confidence: 95%
The underlying network would then determine the most appropriate path and resources.

While such capabilities are beyond the current scope of RADIANT, they illustrate a potential future direction.


Towards a Network Orchestration Layer

A longer-term extension of the Contact Plan as a Service concept would be the introduction of a RADIANT Network Orchestrator.

Such a system could potentially manage:

Contact Information

Scheduled contacts
Predicted visibility windows
Link characteristics
Network Topology

Reachability information
Network maps
Relay relationships
Policy Management

Traffic priorities
Service classes
Resource allocation
Security Services

Node identities
Trust relationships
Authentication policies
Monitoring and Telemetry

Link performance
Network utilisation
Delivery statistics
This would position RADIANT as more than a collection of DTN nodes and instead as a platform for coordinating DTN operations.

Again, this should be viewed as a possible future evolution rather than a near-term objective.


Potential Benefits

Should such an extension eventually be pursued, several potential benefits could emerge.

Interoperability

Multiple DTN implementations could coexist within a common operational framework.

Experimentation

Researchers could compare implementations and routing strategies without modifying applications.

Education

Amateur operators would gain experience not only with DTN protocols but also with the operational concepts required for future space networks.

Future Relevance

The platform could provide a useful environment for investigating networking concepts likely to appear in future lunar and cislunar communications architectures.


Relationship to the Existing Project

It is important to emphasise that these ideas do not replace the current RADIANT objectives.

The current development stages remain focused on:

Building DTN capability within amateur radio.
Evaluating standards-based networking technologies.
Developing practical operational experience.
Establishing a robust experimental platform.
The concepts described in this paper should therefore be viewed as a potential future extension that builds upon successful completion of those goals.

The proposed evolution can be summarised as:

Current Vision
--------------
Amateur Radio + DTN

Possible Future Vision
----------------------
Amateur Radio + DTN + Network Orchestration
The latter depends entirely upon the success and lessons learned from the former.


Conclusion

RADIANT was conceived as a practical testbed for exploring Delay-Tolerant Networking within the amateur radio community and for gaining experience with technologies relevant to future space communications.

As the project progresses, there is an opportunity to consider how the architecture might evolve beyond individual DTN implementations and towards a broader operational framework.

Concepts such as:

DTN implementation abstraction
Contact Plan as a Service
Shared network topology services
Network orchestration
may eventually provide a natural extension of the original vision.

At present, these ideas should be viewed as exploratory proposals intended to stimulate discussion and inform future architectural decisions.

The immediate focus remains the successful implementation of the current RADIANT programme. However, by considering these possibilities now, the project can be developed in a manner that preserves flexibility and supports future growth should such capabilities prove desirable.
